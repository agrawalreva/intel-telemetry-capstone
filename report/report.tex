\begin{abstract}
We design and implement a differentially private query-release system for Intel telemetry: 12 aggregate queries are answered with calibrated Laplace or Analytic Gaussian noise after per-device contribution clipping. Two variants (baseline and advance; same guarantee, advance uses scale caching) are evaluated on mini and full databases. Reported results use the baseline variant. At $\epsilon = 0.01$, Laplace matches or outperforms Gaussian on median relative error, total variation distance, and Spearman rank correlation; at $\epsilon = 1.0$ most queries pass on the full database. The advance variant attains similar utility at lower $\epsilon$ (e.g.\ 0.5). The pipeline is reproducible and provides an auditable $(\epsilon,\delta)$ guarantee.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Hardware and software vendors such as Intel rely on telemetry to understand how products behave in the field. They need aggregate statistics on battery usage, display and power metrics, adoption of features and configurations, and failure or performance patterns by geography, device type, and workload. These insights drive quality improvement, support prioritization, and product planning. To obtain them, vendors collect event logs and system attributes from participating devices: each record is associated with a device identifier (typically a GUID), and data is shipped to central systems for aggregation and analysis. The resulting datasets are large, high-dimensional, and user-level, which makes them valuable for analytics but also sensitive. Releasing aggregate statistics, even after stripping direct identifiers, can still enable re-identification or linkage when combined with other information. The tension between the need for actionable insights and the obligation to protect individuals is the problem we address.

In this work we apply \emph{differentially private query release} to Intel telemetry. Rather than releasing raw or lightly anonymized data, we answer a fixed set of 12 analytical queries by adding calibrated noise to their outputs, after bounding each device's contribution via clipping. We implement two standard mechanisms, the Laplace and the Analytic Gaussian mechanism, and evaluate the tradeoff between privacy parameters and the utility of the released answers. The report is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:methods} (Methods) describes the data and reporting layer, pipeline structure, global privacy model, evaluation metrics, budget allocation, and the detailed design of each query. Section~\ref{sec:results} (Results) presents experimental results on mini and full databases, Laplace vs Gaussian comparison, and best-epsilon selection. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} (Discussion) interprets the privacy-utility tradeoff, practical implications, limitations, and threat model. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} (Conclusion) summarises the contribution and impact.

\subsection{Motivation}
\label{sec:motivation}

Organizations that collect telemetry at scale need aggregate statistics for collaboration, benchmarking, and research while protecting individual users; removal of direct identifiers alone does not prevent inference from rich behavioral traces. Query-based release under Differential Privacy gives analysts noisy answers to predefined queries with a formal guarantee that the probability of any output changes by at most a factor $e^\epsilon$ (and an additive $\delta$ when applicable) when one user's data is added or removed. Implementing this in practice requires defining the unit of privacy (here, one GUID), deriving and bounding each query's global sensitivity, choosing a mechanism and calibrating noise to that sensitivity, and accounting for composition when multiple queries are answered. This work does that for a large telemetry corpus and a fixed set of 12 analytical queries. An alternative would be private synthetic data generation: learn a DP model of the full data distribution, generate a synthetic dataset with the same schema, and release it so that analysts can run arbitrary queries without further privacy cost. That approach offers flexibility and a one-time budget, but it fits our setting poorly. Our telemetry is large (order of terabytes), high-dimensional, and sparse, and many queries join dozens of columns (e.g., web category usage by persona, country, and CPU). Modelling the full joint distribution under DP is extremely demanding at that scale, and synthetic data tends either to over-smooth, to miss rare but important patterns, or to consume a large budget for the whole distribution instead of the summaries we need. Because we have a fixed set of 12 well-defined aggregate queries and an enterprise reporting use case, we adopt query release instead. We spend the privacy budget only on those 12 answers, bound sensitivity tightly per GUID and per query, calibrate noise to each metric type (counts, averages, histograms), and allocate epsilon per query by importance. That yields better accuracy for the statistics we actually use and keeps the guarantee auditable and the implementation tractable at scale.

\subsection{Differential Privacy}
\label{sec:dp_definitions}

Differential privacy (DP) is a mathematical definition of what it means for a randomized computation to limit the harm to an individual from participating in a dataset. The idea, due to Dwork and colleagues \cite{dwork2006calibrating,dwork2014promise}, is that the \emph{distribution} of the mechanism's output should change only slightly when one person's data is added or removed. In that way, an adversary who sees the output cannot confidently tell whether any one individual was present, so participation carries bounded privacy risk. Formally, a mechanism $\mathcal{M}$ is differentially private if for every pair of datasets that differ in one person's records, and for every subset $S$ of possible outputs, the probability $\Pr[\mathcal{M}(D) \in S]$ is close to $\Pr[\mathcal{M}(D') \in S]$. The standard way to quantify ``close'' is with two parameters: $\epsilon \geq 0$ (a multiplicative bound) and $\delta \geq 0$ (an additive slack). The central inequality is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dpdef_highlevel}
\Pr[\mathcal{M}(D) \in S] \;\leq\; e^{\epsilon} \cdot \Pr[\mathcal{M}(D') \in S] \;+\; \delta.
\end{equation}
When $\delta = 0$, the guarantee is \emph{pure} $\epsilon$-differential privacy: the output distribution changes by at most a factor $e^\epsilon$ when one person's data is removed. Smaller $\epsilon$ and $\delta$ mean stronger privacy; in practice $\epsilon$ is often chosen in a range from about 0.01 (very strong) to a few units (weaker but more accurate).

We now make this precise and then state the sensitivity and mechanisms we use.

\paragraph{Neighboring datasets.}
We use \emph{user-level} (device-level) differential privacy. Two datasets $D$ and $D'$ are \emph{neighboring}, written $D \sim D'$, if one can be obtained from the other by adding or removing all records belonging to a single GUID. So the ``one person'' whose presence or absence we protect is one device's entire contribution to the dataset.

\paragraph{Formal definition.}
A randomized mechanism $\mathcal{M}$ with range $\mathcal{R}$ satisfies \emph{$(\epsilon,\delta)$-differential privacy} if for all neighboring $D$, $D'$ and all measurable $S \subseteq \mathcal{R}$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dpdef}
\Pr[\mathcal{M}(D) \in S] \leq e^{\epsilon} \, \Pr[\mathcal{M}(D') \in S] + \delta.
\end{equation}
When $\delta = 0$, we say $\mathcal{M}$ satisfies \emph{pure} $\epsilon$-DP \cite{dwork2014promise}.

\paragraph{Global sensitivity.}
To add the right amount of noise, we need a bound on how much the \emph{query function} $f$ can change when one person's data changes. The \emph{global sensitivity} of $f$ is the maximum change over all neighboring datasets. For a real-valued $f$,
\begin{equation}
\Delta f \;=\; \max_{D \sim D'} \, \bigl| f(D) - f(D') \bigr|.
\end{equation}
For a vector-valued $f$ with values in $\mathbb{R}^d$, we use two norms: the $\ell_1$ global sensitivity $\Delta_1 f = \max_{D \sim D'} \sum_{i=1}^d |f_i(D) - f_i(D')|$ (for the Laplace mechanism) and the $\ell_2$ global sensitivity $\Delta_2 f = \max_{D \sim D'} \|f(D) - f(D')\|_2$ (for the Gaussian mechanism). Sensitivity must be a worst-case, data-independent bound. For the query types in this work we use: \textbf{Count:} $\Delta f = 1$. \textbf{Sum with per-GUID clipping at $C$:} $\Delta f = C$. \textbf{Average} over at least $k_{\min}$ GUIDs with per-GUID contribution clipped to $C$: $\Delta f = C/k_{\min}$. \textbf{Histogram} (one bin per GUID): each GUID affects at most one bin by 1, so the $\ell_1$ sensitivity of the full vector is 1 and the $\ell_2$ sensitivity is 1.

\paragraph{Laplace mechanism.}
For a function $f$ with $\ell_1$ global sensitivity $\Delta_1 f$, the Laplace mechanism \cite{dwork2006calibrating,dwork2014promise} releases
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{M}(D) \;=\; f(D) + \text{Lap}(0, b), \qquad b \;=\; \frac{\Delta_1 f}{\epsilon}.
\end{equation}
Here $\text{Lap}(0,b)$ denotes a draw from the Laplace distribution with mean 0 and scale $b$ (so the variance is $2b^2$). This mechanism satisfies pure $\epsilon$-DP. For vector-valued $f$, we use the $\ell_1$ sensitivity of the vector and add independent $\text{Lap}(0, \Delta_1 f/\epsilon)$ to each coordinate; the resulting mechanism is $\epsilon$-DP \cite{dwork2014promise}.

\paragraph{Gaussian and Analytic Gaussian mechanism.}
For $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP, the Gaussian mechanism releases $f(D) + Z$ where $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)$. The classical analysis gives a sufficient condition $\sigma \geq (\Delta_2 f / \epsilon) \sqrt{2 \ln(1.25/\delta)}$, which is not tight. The \emph{Analytic Gaussian mechanism} \cite{balle2018improving} calibrates $\sigma$ via the exact $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP condition. For a scalar output with $\ell_2$ sensitivity $\Delta$, the mechanism is $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP if and only if
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:analytic_gaussian}
\Phi\left( \frac{\Delta}{2\sigma} - \frac{\epsilon\sigma}{\Delta} \right) \;-\; e^\epsilon \cdot \Phi\left( -\frac{\Delta}{2\sigma} - \frac{\epsilon\sigma}{\Delta} \right) \;\leq\; \delta,
\end{equation}
where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF \cite{balle2018improving}. We find the smallest $\sigma$ satisfying this inequality (e.g., by binary search). For vector-valued $f$, we use the $\ell_2$ sensitivity $\Delta_2 f$ in place of $\Delta$. This analytic calibration yields a smaller $\sigma$ than the classical bound for the same $(\epsilon,\delta)$, hence better utility.

\paragraph{Composition and post-processing.}
If $\mathcal{M}_1$ is $(\epsilon_1,\delta_1)$-DP and $\mathcal{M}_2$ is $(\epsilon_2,\delta_2)$-DP, their sequential composition is $(\epsilon_1+\epsilon_2, \delta_1+\delta_2)$-DP (basic composition \cite{dwork2014promise}). We allocate a total budget $\epsilon_{\text{total}}$ across the 12 queries and state the composed guarantee explicitly (Section~\ref{sec:budget}). Post-processing does not weaken the guarantee: if $\mathcal{M}$ is $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP and $g$ is any deterministic function, then $g \circ \mathcal{M}$ is $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP. So clamping outputs to non-negative values or re-normalising percentages after adding noise costs no extra privacy.

\paragraph{Report Noisy Max.}
For queries that release the identity of the maximum over a set of counts (e.g., the most popular browser per country), we use Report Noisy Max \cite{dwork2014promise}: add independent $\text{Lap}(0, 1/\epsilon)$ to each count (each count has sensitivity 1), then release the index of the maximum noisy count. The whole selection is $\epsilon$-DP.

\subsection{Telemetry Data}
\label{sec:telemetry_data}

The data used in this work is Intel telemetry accessed through Globus. The source data consists of 23 tables across two schemas: \textbf{university\_analysis\_pad} and \textbf{university\_prod}. Each row is associated with a device identifier (GUID); multiple rows per GUID are common. The data exhibits heavy-tailed distributions; hence a small fraction of GUIDs can contribute disproportionately to aggregates. We therefore \emph{clip} per-GUID contributions before aggregation so that global sensitivity is bounded by a fixed constant. Concretely, we cap quantities (including the number of power-on events per GUID and the total duration per GUID in a given category) at constants $C_1$, $C_2$, and similar bounds, as specified per query in Section~\ref{sec:methods}. These caps are chosen so that global sensitivity is bounded in the worst case (e.g., when a group has exactly $k_{\min}$ devices) while still covering typical or high-percentile usage (e.g., 600\,s for display duration, 720\,min for daily sleep time, 10\,W for per-user power). The aggregate query is then evaluated on the clipped view and the DP mechanism is applied to the query output. Clipping is a deterministic pre-processing step; global sensitivity is computed with respect to the clipped query function only.

We use a specialized reporting layer of 22 pre-aggregated tables built from the raw tables via a SQL build script, and we run 12 benchmark queries covering battery usage by geography and CPU family, display device vendor market share, browser popularity by country, OS-level MODS blockers, RAM utilization, persona-level web category usage, and process power rankings. The 12 query results are exported as baseline (non-private) CSVs. We then apply both the Laplace and the Analytic Gaussian mechanism to every query (Section~\ref{sec:global_model}), with epsilon allocated via sequential composition (Section~\ref{sec:budget}). Post-processing consists of clamping numeric outputs to non-negative values where required and re-normalising percentage columns to sum to 100\% where applicable. The pipeline is developed and validated on a subsample database (approximately 5\,GB) before execution on the full dataset (on the order of tens of GB; see repository README for current size estimates).

\subsection{Applications}
\label{sec:applications}

Privacy-preserving query release over telemetry supports usage metrics and feature adoption by segment, performance and health monitoring by geography or hardware family, market share and ranking statistics, and anomaly detection (identification of segments with unusually high error rates or power draw). Analysts receive noisy answers suitable for trend analysis and prioritisation without access to raw user-level data. The same framework applies to other telemetry or event-log datasets where user-level differential privacy is required.

\subsection{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Differential privacy was introduced in \cite{dwork2006calibrating} and is treated in depth in \cite{dwork2014promise}, which covers the Laplace and Gaussian mechanisms, global sensitivity, and composition. The Analytic Gaussian mechanism of \cite{balle2018improving} improves noise calibration for $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP and is well suited to query release when many numeric outputs are perturbed. Query answering under DP in database and analytics settings requires bounding sensitivity (typically via clipping or projection) and allocating privacy budget across queries. In this work all guarantees follow from standard mechanisms, global sensitivity derived from clipping and query structure, and explicit sequential composition.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

This section describes how we obtain the data, how the pipeline is structured, the global privacy model (unit of privacy, sensitivity, mechanisms, composition), the choice and meaning of the three evaluation metrics (median relative error, total variation distance, and Spearman rank correlation) and their assignment to queries, the allocation of the privacy budget across queries, and the use of multiple epsilon values to trace the privacy-utility curve. We then give the full mathematical definition, sensitivity, mechanism, and evaluation metric for each of the 12 queries (Sections~\ref{sec:q1} to \ref{sec:q12}).

\subsection{Data Access and Reporting Layer}
\label{sec:data_access}

Raw Intel telemetry is stored in 23 tables across two schemas. We build two DuckDB databases: a subsample with capped table sizes for development, and a full database for production. A build step (SQL script \texttt{00\_build\_reporting\_tables.sql}) constructs 22 reporting tables that resolve joins and normalise column names. The 12 benchmark queries are executed against this reporting layer; their results are exported and serve as the non-private baseline. Per-query clipping is applied in SQL so that each device's contribution is bounded before aggregation; global sensitivity is derived from these clipping constants and the query structure only.

\subsection{Project and Pipeline Structure}
\label{sec:project_structure}

The pipeline proceeds in six stages. (1) \textbf{Data and reporting layer:} raw telemetry is loaded into DuckDB; a build step produces 22 reporting tables under a single schema. (2) \textbf{Baseline export:} the 12 benchmark queries are run with per-GUID clipping applied in SQL; results are the non-private baseline. (3) \textbf{Differential privacy release:} we run the Laplace and Analytic Gaussian mechanisms at each $\epsilon$ in a fixed grid, with one noise draw per output and a fixed random seed for reproducibility; two implementation variants (baseline and advance) are available (Section~\ref{sec:mechanism_variants}). (4) \textbf{Evaluation:} per-query statistics (worst/best value, pass rate) and Laplace vs Gaussian comparison are computed across $\epsilon$. (5) \textbf{Best-epsilon selection:} a utility-preservation score (relative to baseline at $\epsilon = \infty$) selects a recommended $\epsilon$ per mechanism; Tier 1 is the smallest $\epsilon$ where mean utility preserved reaches 80\%, with a Tier 2 fallback to the $\epsilon$ with highest score if none reach that target. (6) \textbf{Visualization:} privacy-utility curves, mechanism comparison, pass rate, best-epsilon summary, and Pareto frontier are produced from the evaluation outputs. This structure keeps sensitivity at the query level, mechanisms in dedicated modules, and evaluation independent of the release step.

\subsection{Mechanism Variants: Baseline and Advance}
\label{sec:mechanism_variants}

The pipeline implements each mechanism (Laplace and Analytic Gaussian) in two variants: \textbf{baseline} and \textbf{advance}. They are \emph{mathematically identical}: for a given query, $\epsilon$, and sensitivity, both produce the same noise distribution and the same privacy guarantee. The difference is how the noise scale is computed at run time.

In the \textbf{baseline} variant, the noise scale is recomputed from scratch for every (query, column, $\epsilon$) triple. For Laplace, the scale is $b = \Delta_1 f / \epsilon$ (closed form). For the Analytic Gaussian, $\sigma$ is found by binary search so that the exact $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP condition holds \cite{balle2018improving}; that search is run separately for each combination of sensitivity and $\epsilon$ encountered in the loop over queries and columns. This is straightforward and easy to verify, but when many queries share the same sensitivity (or a small set of sensitivities), the same binary search is repeated many times.

In the \textbf{advance} variant, a \emph{scale cache} is built once before the main loop: for each unique pair (sensitivity, $\epsilon$), the Laplace scale $b$ or the Gaussian $\sigma$ is computed once and stored. During the release step, the implementation looks up the precomputed scale instead of recomputing it. Because the number of distinct (sensitivity, $\epsilon$) pairs is much smaller than the number of (query, column, $\epsilon$) triples, the advance variant typically runs about 2--4$\times$ faster than the baseline with identical numerical output. It is therefore preferred for production or for running the full grid of $\epsilon$ values on large data.

We report main results and figures for the \textbf{baseline} variant at $\epsilon = 1.0$ (Section~\ref{sec:results}). The \textbf{advance} variant often attains similar utility at a lower $\epsilon$ (e.g.\ 0.5) on the same data, so it can offer a stronger privacy guarantee when used in deployment; that comparison is discussed in Section~\ref{sec:disc_interpretation}.

\subsection{Global Privacy Model}
\label{sec:global_model}

\textbf{Unit of privacy.} We use \emph{user-level} differential privacy: the unit of privacy is one GUID. Neighboring datasets $D$ and $D'$ differ in all rows associated with exactly one GUID (i.e., adding or removing one user's entire activity).

\textbf{Contribution bounding.} Before aggregation we apply per-GUID contribution clipping so that the influence of any single GUID on the query output is bounded. Clipping is a deterministic, data-independent pre-processing step; global sensitivity is computed with respect to the clipped query function.

\textbf{Sensitivity.} For each query we derive the global sensitivity $\Delta f$ (or $\Delta_1 f$, $\Delta_2 f$ for vector outputs) from the clipping bounds and the query structure.

\textbf{Mechanisms.} We run \emph{both} the Laplace and the Analytic Gaussian mechanism on every query so that we can compare their privacy-utility tradeoff in the evaluation. The per-query ``assignment'' in the table and subsections indicates which mechanism is \emph{preferred} for that query type (because it typically yields better utility for the same privacy budget), not which one we run. Laplace is preferred for count queries, histograms, Report Noisy Max, and low-dimensional aggregates. The Analytic Gaussian is preferred for high-dimensional or multi-column outputs (Queries 7, 8, 9) because its analytic calibration yields a smaller $\sigma$ than the classical bound for the same $(\epsilon,\delta)$, improving utility when the budget is spread over many dimensions. Concretely:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Laplace mechanism} (pure $\epsilon$-DP): release $f(D) + \text{Lap}(0, b)$ with $b = \Delta_1 f / \epsilon$.
    \item \textbf{Analytic Gaussian mechanism} ($(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP): release $f(D) + \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ where $\sigma$ is calibrated via \cite{balle2018improving}. We fix $\delta = 10^{-6}$ per query.
\end{itemize}
Preferred assignment: Laplace for queries 1 to 6 and 10 to 12; Analytic Gaussian for queries 7, 8, and 9.

\textbf{Composition.} The 12 queries are answered under \emph{sequential composition}: if query $i$ uses $(\epsilon_i, \delta_i)$-DP, the total guarantee is $(\sum_{i=1}^{12} \epsilon_i,\, \sum_{i=1}^{12} \delta_i)$-DP. We allocate a total budget $\epsilon_{\text{total}}$ so that $\sum_{i=1}^{12} \epsilon_i \leq \epsilon_{\text{total}}$. When a single query releases multiple numeric outputs, we either split that query's $\epsilon_i$ across outputs or treat the output as a vector and apply the vector-valued mechanism once.

\textbf{Post-processing.} Deterministic post-processing (clamping to non-negative, re-normalising percentages to sum to 100\%) does not consume privacy budget and preserves $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP.

\subsection{Query 1: Battery Power On Geographic Summary}
\label{sec:q1}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_batt\_dc\_events (guid, num\_power\_ons, duration\_mins); reporting.system\_sysinfo\_unique\_normalized (guid, countryname\_normalized).

\textbf{Research question.} Which countries have abnormally long battery discharge durations?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} Let $D$ be the dataset of per-GUID battery discharge events. For each country $c$, define the clipped per-GUID average of duration with $C = 60$ minutes:
\[
x_g^c = \text{clip}(\text{avg}_{\text{session}}(\text{duration\_mins}_g), C), \qquad
f(D)_c = \frac{1}{n_c} \sum_{g \in c} x_g^c, \quad n_c = |\{g : \text{country}(g) = c\}|,
\]
restricted to groups with $n_c \geq k_{\min} = 100$. Output: a vector of (country, avg\_duration) pairs, ordered by avg\_duration.

\textbf{Contribution bounding.} For each GUID: clip total \texttt{num\_power\_ons} to $C_1 = 10$ and total \texttt{duration\_mins} to $C_2 = 60$. Averages use $k_{\min} = 100$.

\textbf{Sensitivity.} Adding or removing one GUID changes $f(D)_c$ by at most $C/n_c$. Worst case over neighboring datasets is $n_c = k_{\min}$, so \emph{global} sensitivity is $\Delta = C/k_{\min} = 60/100 = 0.60$ (we use only this; no data-dependent reduction). Per country we release one scalar (avg\_duration); Laplace scale $b = \Delta/\epsilon_1$.

\textbf{Mechanism:} Laplace: add $\text{Lap}(0, \Delta/\epsilon_1)$ to avg\_duration per country. Single draw per output. Epsilon: $\epsilon_1$ (medium).

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Median relative error (on avg\_duration).

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$; ranking and z-scores are deterministic functions of the noisy output.

\subsection{Query 2: Battery On Duration by CPU Family and Generation}
\label{sec:q2}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_cpu\_metadata (guid, marketcodename, cpugen); reporting.system\_batt\_dc\_events (guid, duration\_mins).

\textbf{Research question.} Which CPU family-generation combinations have abnormal battery duration?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} For each group $c = (\text{marketcodename}, \text{cpugen})$ with $C = 60$:
\[
x_g^c = \text{clip}(\text{avg}_{\text{event}}(\text{duration\_mins}_g), C), \qquad
f(D)_c = \frac{1}{n_c} \sum_{g \in c} x_g^c, \quad n_c \geq k_{\min} = 100.
\]
Output ordered by avg\_duration.

\textbf{Contribution bounding.} Clip each GUID's total \texttt{duration\_mins} to $C = 60$.

\textbf{Sensitivity.} One GUID changes the group average by at most $C/n_c$; worst case $n_c = k_{\min}$ gives $\Delta = C/k_{\min} = 60/100 = 0.60$. Laplace scale $b = \Delta/\epsilon_2$.

\textbf{Mechanism:} Laplace: add $\text{Lap}(0, \Delta/\epsilon_2)$ to avg\_duration per group. Single draw. Epsilon: $\epsilon_2$ (medium).

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Median relative error (on avg\_duration).

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$.

\subsection{Query 3: Display Devices Connection Type Resolution Durations AC/DC}
\label{sec:q3}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_display\_devices (guid, connection\_type, resolution\_height, resolution\_width, duration\_ac, duration\_dc).

\textbf{Research question.} Which display connection-type and resolution combinations have the longest AC usage time?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} For each group $c = (\text{connection\_type}, \text{resolution})$ with $C = 600$ seconds (10 minutes):
\[
x_g^c = \text{clip}(\text{avg}_{\text{session}}(\text{duration\_ac}_g), C), \qquad
f(D)_c = \frac{1}{n_c} \sum_{g \in c} x_g^c, \quad n_c \geq k_{\min} = 50.
\]
Output ranked by $f(D)_c$ descending.

\textbf{Contribution bounding.} Clip per-GUID \texttt{duration\_ac} and \texttt{duration\_dc} to $C = 600$ seconds.

\textbf{Sensitivity.} One GUID changes the group average by at most $C/n_c$; worst case $n_c = k_{\min}$ gives $\Delta = C/k_{\min} = 600/50 = 12.0$ seconds. Laplace scale $b = \Delta/\epsilon_3$ for the released average\_duration\_on\_ac.

\textbf{Mechanism:} Laplace: add $\text{Lap}(0, \Delta/\epsilon_3)$ to average\_duration\_on\_ac per group. Single draw. Epsilon: $\epsilon_3$ (smaller).

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Spearman $\rho$ (rank correlation on average\_duration\_on\_ac).

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$; ranking is deterministic post-processing.

\subsection{Query 4: Display Devices Vendors Percentage}
\label{sec:q4}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_display\_devices (guid, vendor\_name).

\textbf{Research question.} What fraction of systems use each display vendor?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} Let $N = \sum_v n_v$ be the total number of distinct GUIDs. For each vendor $v$:
\[
n_v = |\{g : \text{vendor}(g) = v\}|, \qquad f(D)_v = (n_v / N) \times 100 \text{ (percentage of systems)}.
\]
Output: vector (vendor, percentage) summing to 100\%.

\textbf{Contribution bounding.} One GUID contributes to exactly one vendor; adding/removing one GUID changes $n_v$ by at most 1 and $N$ by 1. Change in percentage is bounded; we use the conservative $\Delta = 1.0$ (percentage point) for the released statistic.

\textbf{Sensitivity.} $\Delta = 1.0$. Laplace scale $b = \Delta/\epsilon_4$ per vendor percentage.

\textbf{Mechanism:} Laplace: add $\text{Lap}(0, \Delta/\epsilon_4)$ to each vendor's percentage (or to counts then form percentages as post-processing). Single draw. Epsilon: $\epsilon_4$ (smaller).

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Median relative error (on percentage\_of\_systems).

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$; re-normalise to sum to 100\% (deterministic).

\subsection{Query 5: MODS Blockers by OS Name and Codename}
\label{sec:q5}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_mods\_top\_blocker\_hist (guid, dt);
reporting.system\_os\_codename\_history (guid, min\_ts, max\_ts, os\_name, os\_codename).
Join on GUID and time windows.

\textbf{Research question.} Which OS versions generate the most MODS blocker events per system?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} For each group $c = (\text{os\_name}, \text{os\_codename})$ with $C = 50$:
\[
e_g^c = \text{clip}(\text{COUNT}(\text{events for } g \in c), C),
\]
\[
f(D)_c = \frac{1}{n_c} \sum_{g \in c} e_g^c \text{ (avg.\ clipped entries per system)}, \quad n_c \geq k_{\min} = 10.
\]

\textbf{Contribution bounding.} Clip each GUID's entry count to $C = 50$ per group.

\textbf{Sensitivity.} One GUID changes the group average by at most $C/n_c$; worst case $n_c = k_{\min}$ gives $\Delta = C/k_{\min} = 50/10 = 5.0$. Laplace scale $b = \Delta/\epsilon_5$.

\textbf{Mechanism:} Laplace: add $\text{Lap}(0, \Delta/\epsilon_5)$ to entries\_per\_system per group. Single draw. Epsilon: $\epsilon_5$ (smaller).

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Median relative error (on entries\_per\_system).

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$.

\subsection{Query 6: Most Popular Browser in Each Country by System Count}
\label{sec:q6}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_sysinfo\_unique\_normalized (guid, countryname\_normalized); reporting.system\_web\_cat\_usage (guid, browser).

\textbf{Research question.} Which browser is most used in each country?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} For each country $c$ and browser $b$:
\[
\text{count}(c,b) = |\{g : \text{country}(g)=c,\, \text{browser}(g)=b\}|, \qquad f(D)_c = \arg\max_b \text{count}(c,b).
\]
Output: one (country, browser) pair per country.

\textbf{Sensitivity.} Each $\text{count}(c,b)$ has global sensitivity $\Delta = 1$ (one GUID changes one count by 1).

\textbf{Mechanism:} \textbf{Report Noisy Max.} Per country: add $\text{Lap}(0, 1/\epsilon_6)$ to each browser count; release $\arg\max_b (\text{count}(c,b) + \text{noise}_b)$. This satisfies $\epsilon_6$-DP per country. We allocate $\epsilon_6$ per country (or account for composition across countries in the global budget).

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Spearman $\rho$ (rank correlation on winner ordering per country).

\textbf{Post-processing:} None beyond argmax.

\subsection{Query 7: On/Off MODS Sleep Summary by CPU Market Codename and Generation}
\label{sec:q7}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_on\_off\_suspend\_time\_day (guid, on\_time, off\_time, mods\_time, sleep\_time); reporting.system\_cpu\_metadata (guid, marketcodename, cpugen).

\textbf{Research question.} Which CPU families spend an abnormally high fraction of time in Modern Standby (MODS)?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} For each group $c = (\text{marketcodename}, \text{cpugen})$ with $C = 720$ minutes per day (12 hours):
\[
x_g^c = \text{clip}(\text{avg}_{\text{day}}(\text{mods\_time}_g), C), \qquad
f(D)_c = \frac{1}{n_c} \sum_{g \in c} x_g^c \text{ (avg\_modern\_sleep\_time)}, \quad n_c \geq k_{\min} = 100.
\]
Output includes multiple time columns; the evaluation metric uses only avg\_modern\_sleep\_time.

\textbf{Contribution bounding.} Clip per-GUID total time per category to $C = 720$ minutes per day. For the key column (avg\_modern\_sleep\_time): $\Delta = C/k_{\min} = 720/100 = 7.2$ minutes. We add noise to \emph{every} released numeric column (or treat the release as one vector with joint L1/L2 sensitivity and one mechanism call).

\textbf{Sensitivity.} $\Delta = 7.2$ for the average; if releasing multiple columns, joint sensitivity is used (e.g., L2 over the vector of sensitivities).

\textbf{Mechanism:} Laplace or Analytic Gaussian: add noise with scale $\Delta/\epsilon_7$ (Laplace) or $\sigma(\Delta,\epsilon_7,\delta)$ (Gaussian) to the released statistics. Single draw. Epsilon: $\epsilon_7$ (medium).

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Total variation distance (mean over sleep percentage columns).

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$; re-normalise percentages if computed.

\subsection{Query 8: Persona Web Category Usage Analysis}
\label{sec:q8}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_sysinfo\_unique\_normalized (guid, persona);
reporting.system\_web\_cat\_pivot\_duration (guid, category duration columns).

\textbf{Research question.} How does web browsing time distribute across 28 content categories for each user persona?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} For each persona $p$ and category $k$: $C_{\text{days}} = 365$, $C_{\text{dur}} = 3{,}600{,}000$ ms;
\[
\text{days}_g = \text{clip}(\text{COUNT}(\text{days for } g), C_{\text{days}}), \quad
\text{cat}_g^k = \text{clip}(\text{SUM}(\text{category}_k \text{ duration for } g), C_{\text{dur}}),
\]
\[
f(D)_{p,k} = \sum_{g \in p} \text{days}_g \cdot (\text{cat}_g^k / \text{total}_g) \big/ \sum_{g \in p} \text{days}_g.
\]
Output: 28-dimensional (fraction or percentage) vector per persona, summing to $\sim$100\%.

\textbf{Contribution bounding.} Per-GUID contributions are clipped; each category output has bounded sensitivity (e.g., $\Delta \leq 1$ per column in normalised form). We use either: (1) \textbf{Split budget:} allocate $\epsilon_{\text{per column}} = \epsilon_8/28$ per column so the total composition is $\epsilon_8$; or (2) \textbf{Single vector release:} treat the 28-dimensional output as one vector-valued query, compute the $\ell_2$ (or $\ell_1$) global sensitivity of the full vector, and apply the mechanism once with that sensitivity and budget $\epsilon_8$.

\textbf{Sensitivity.} Per column (if splitting): $\Delta_j \leq 1$; then $\epsilon_j = \epsilon_8/28$. For a single vector release: $\Delta_2 = \sqrt{\sum_j \Delta_j^2}$ (e.g., $\sqrt{28}$ if $\Delta_j = 1$).

\textbf{Mechanism:} Laplace with $\epsilon/28$ per column, or Analytic Gaussian on the full vector with $\epsilon_8$ and L2 sensitivity $\Delta_2$. Single draw per output (per column or per vector).

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Total variation distance (mean across persona category distributions).

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$; re-normalise each persona's row to sum to 100\%; optional Dirichlet smoothing to avoid zeros in KL.

\subsection{Query 9: Package Power by Country}
\label{sec:q9}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_sysinfo\_unique\_normalized (guid, countryname\_normalized); reporting.system\_hw\_pkg\_power (guid, nrs, mean).

\textbf{Research question.} Which countries have abnormally high CPU package power consumption?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} For each country $c$ with $C = 50$ W:
\[
x_g^c = \text{clip}(\text{SUM}(\text{nrs} \cdot \text{mean})/\text{SUM}(\text{nrs}) \text{ for } g, C), \qquad
f(D)_c = \frac{1}{n_c} \sum_{g \in c} x_g^c, \quad n_c \geq k_{\min} = 100.
\]

\textbf{Contribution bounding.} Clip per-GUID average power to $C = 50$ W.

\textbf{Sensitivity.} One GUID changes the group average by at most $C/n_c$; worst case $n_c = k_{\min}$ gives $\Delta = C/k_{\min} = 50/100 = 0.50$ W. Laplace scale $b = \Delta/\epsilon_9$.

\textbf{Mechanism:} Laplace or Analytic Gaussian: add noise with scale $\Delta/\epsilon_9$. Single draw. Epsilon: $\epsilon_9$ (medium).

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Median relative error (on avg\_pkg\_power\_consumed).

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$.

\subsection{Query 10: Popular Browsers by Count Usage Percentage}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_web\_cat\_usage (guid, browser, duration\_ms).

\textbf{Research question.} What are the global market shares of browsers by system count (and optionally instance count, duration)?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} For each browser $b$:

n_b = |\{g : \text{browser}(g) = b\}|, \quad N = \sum_b n_b, \qquad f(D)_b = (n_b / N) \times 100 \text{ (percent\_systems)}.

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$; re-normalise to sum to 100\%.

\subsection{Query 11: RAM Utilization Histogram}
\label{sec:q11}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_memory\_utilization (guid, sysinfo\_ram, nrs, avg\_percentage\_used).

\textbf{Research question.} How does average RAM utilisation distribute across RAM size bins?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} For each RAM bin $b$ (e.g., ram\_gb value) with $C = 100\%$:
\[
x_g^b = \text{clip}(\text{avg}_{\text{measurement}}(\text{pct\_used}_g), C), \qquad
f(D)_b = \frac{1}{n_b} \sum_{g \in b} x_g^b, \quad n_b \geq k_{\min} = 50.

Output: (ram\_gb, avg\_percentage\_used) per bin.

\subsection{Query 12: Ranked Process Classifications}
\label{sec:q12}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_mods\_power\_consumption (user\_id, total\_power\_consumption).

\textbf{Research question.} Which user IDs consume the most total CPU power across all processes?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} For each user $u$ with $C = 10$ (watts):
\[
f(D)_u = \text{clip}\Bigl(\sum_{\text{process}} \text{power\_consumption}_{u,\text{process}}, C\Bigr).
\]
Output: ranked list of (user\_id, total\_power\_consumption) by $f(D)_u$ descending. This is a \emph{SUM} query (not an average), so one user's removal changes that user's output by at most $C$.

\textbf{Sensitivity.} $\Delta = C = 10$ per user (global sensitivity of the sum). Laplace scale $b = \Delta/\epsilon_{12}$ per user. Composition: one vector release over all users; total $\epsilon_{12}$ for the query.

\textbf{Mechanism:} Laplace: add $\text{Lap}(0, \Delta/\epsilon_{12})$ to total\_power\_consumption per user. Single draw. Epsilon: $\epsilon_{12}$ (moderate).

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Spearman $\rho$ (rank correlation on total\_power\_consumption).

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$; ranking is deterministic post-processing.

\subsection{Privacy Budget Allocation}
\label{sec:budget}

We fix a total privacy budget $\epsilon_{\text{total}}$ and allocate per-query budgets $\epsilon_1, \ldots, \epsilon_{12}$ such that
\[
\sum_{i=1}^{12} \epsilon_i \leq \epsilon_{\text{total}}.
\]
The allocation reflects the sensitivity and dimensionality of each query: Query 8 (28-dimensional) receives a larger share (about 20\% of $\epsilon_{\text{total}}$), with $\epsilon_8$ consumed either by splitting $\epsilon_8/28$ per category column (basic composition) or by one vector-valued release with joint sensitivity (Section~\ref{sec:q8}). Core usage queries Q1, Q2, Q7, Q9 receive a medium allocation; simple histograms and percentages (Q4, Q5, Q10, Q11) receive a smaller allocation; ranking and Report Noisy Max (Q6, Q12) receive a moderate allocation; Q3 receives a smaller allocation. For the Analytic Gaussian mechanism we fix $\delta = 10^{-6}$ per query. Under sequential composition the full system satisfies $(\epsilon_{\text{total}}, \delta_{\text{total}})$-DP with $\epsilon_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^{12} \epsilon_i$ and $\delta_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^{12} \delta_i = 12 \times 10^{-6}$.

\subsection{Multiple Epsilon Evaluation and the Privacy-Utility Curve}
\label{sec:multi_epsilon}

We run the pipeline at a range of $\epsilon$ values ($\epsilon \in \{0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.5, 1.0, \infty\}$) so that we can trace the privacy-utility curve and choose an operating point for the full database without running every $\epsilon$ on the expensive full run. At each $\epsilon$ we use the same allocation of $\epsilon_{\text{total}}$ across the 12 queries; only the scale of the noise changes. For each query and each $\epsilon$ we compute the corresponding evaluation metric (median relative error, total variation distance, or Spearman rank correlation) and record the gap between the true and DP outputs. This yields a \emph{privacy-utility curve}: gap as a function of $\epsilon$. Typically, smaller $\epsilon$ gives larger gap (more noise, lower utility), and larger $\epsilon$ gives smaller gap (less noise, higher utility). We report results for both the Laplace and the Analytic Gaussian mechanism at each $\epsilon$ so that we can compare the two mechanisms. The ``best'' $\epsilon$ in a given context is the smallest $\epsilon$ at which utility remains acceptable (e.g., mean utility preservation at or above a target); that value represents the strongest privacy guarantee under which the released statistics are still usable. We do not assume a single universal best $\epsilon$; the evaluation is intended to inform the choice of $\epsilon$ (and mechanism) depending on which queries and which gap levels are deemed acceptable for deployment.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

All results and figures in this section use the \textbf{baseline} mechanism variant (Section~\ref{sec:mechanism_variants}). The baseline and advance variants are mathematically identical; the advance variant precomputes noise scales for speed and often achieves similar utility at a lower $\epsilon$ (see Discussion). We report results from the mini (subsample) and full databases. The evaluation compares Laplace and Analytic Gaussian mechanisms across a range of $\epsilon$ values and identifies a recommended $\epsilon$ for production use with the baseline variant.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:exp_setup}

Experiments were run on two database tiers: \textbf{mini}, a subsample with capped table sizes (approximately 5\,GB), and \textbf{full}, the production-scale database. We evaluate all six $\epsilon$ values on mini to trace the privacy-utility curve and to select one $\epsilon$ for the full run; the full database was then run only at that selected $\epsilon$ to avoid the cost of running every $\epsilon$ on the full scale. The epsilon grid was $\epsilon \in \{0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.5, 1.0, \infty\}$. All mechanism runs used a fixed random seed for reproducibility. Results below are for the \textbf{baseline} variant; evaluation summaries and per-query comparison tables were produced for both mini and full. The \textbf{advance} variant is evaluated separately and selects a lower best $\epsilon$ on mini (Section~\ref{sec:disc_interpretation}).

\subsection{Privacy Parameters}
\label{sec:privacy_params}

For the Laplace mechanism we use pure $\epsilon$-DP ($\delta = 0$). For the Analytic Gaussian mechanism we set $\delta = 10^{-6}$ per query; under sequential composition across 12 queries the full system satisfies $(\epsilon_{\text{total}}, 12 \times 10^{-6})$-DP. Sensitivity per query is derived from the clipping constants and $k_{\min}$ (e.g., $\Delta = C/k_{\min}$ for averages, $\Delta = C$ for sums, $\Delta = 1$ for counts and conservative percentage columns).

\subsection{Utility Metrics and Pass Rates}
\label{sec:utility_metrics}

Utility is measured per query by the metric type assigned in Section~\ref{sec:eval_metrics_methods}: median relative error for queries 1, 2, 4, 5, and 9; total variation distance for queries 7, 8, 10, and 11; Spearman rank correlation for queries 3, 6, and 12. Pass thresholds are median relative error $\leq 0.25$, total variation distance $\leq 0.15$, and Spearman $\rho \geq 0.5$. At $\epsilon = 0.01$ (strong privacy), most queries fail the hard threshold; at $\epsilon = 1.0$ or $\infty$, most pass. For example, on the mini database with Laplace, queries 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 7, 8, 9 each achieve their best (lowest median relative error or total variation distance, or highest Spearman rank correlation) at $\epsilon = \infty$; at $\epsilon = 0.01$ the worst-case median relative error reaches 1.0 for several queries and total variation distance reaches about 0.55 to 0.63 for Q7 and Q8. Query 5 (MODS blockers) is particularly sensitive: median relative error at $\epsilon = 0.01$ is 6.76 (Laplace) and 18.16 (Gaussian) due to the small number of groups (11 rows in mini). Query 6 (most popular browser) and query 12 (ranked processes) show near-constant or zero Spearman rank correlation on mini (tied or degenerate rankings).

\subsection{Best Epsilon Selection}
\label{sec:best_epsilon}

We compute a utility-preservation score for each $\epsilon$ by comparing each query's DP metric value to that same query's no-noise baseline at $\epsilon = \infty$; the score is the fraction of baseline utility preserved (scale-agnostic). Selection uses two tiers (as in the repository README): Tier 1 is the smallest $\epsilon$ where mean preservation across queries reaches 80\%; Tier 2, if no $\epsilon$ reaches 80\%, is the $\epsilon$ with the highest mean preservation. On the mini database, no $\epsilon$ reached 80\% (many queries have zero or near-zero baseline), so the logic chose $\epsilon = \textbf{1.0}$ as the best available (highest mean utility preserved) for both Laplace and Gaussian; runner-up was 0.5. Full-database runs were therefore executed at $\epsilon = 1.0$ only, avoiding the cost of running every $\epsilon$ on the full database. At $\epsilon = 1.0$, full-database results show that most queries pass their thresholds under both mechanisms; exceptions include Q4 (vendor percentage) and Q6 (browser winner), which remain difficult due to distribution shape or categorical nature.

\subsection{Full-Database Results at $\epsilon = 1.0$}
\label{sec:full_results}

On the full database at $\epsilon = 1.0$, Laplace achieves median relative error below 0.03 for Q1, Q2, and Q9; Q5 has median relative error 0.11 (Laplace) and 0.22 (Gaussian). Spearman $\rho \geq 0.99$ for Q3; total variation distance 0.044 (Q7) and 0.099 (Q8). Gaussian yields slightly higher median relative error and total variation distance at the same $\epsilon$. Q4 and Q6 do not meet the hard pass threshold on full at $\epsilon = 1.0$ (median relative error 0.66 for Q4 Laplace; Spearman $\rho$ 0.064 for Q6), indicating that either a larger $\epsilon$ or a different mechanism (e.g., Report Noisy Max explicitly for Q6) would be needed for deployment.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Privacy and Utility Tradeoff}
\label{sec:disc_tradeoff}

The results confirm the expected privacy-utility tradeoff: at $\epsilon = 0.01$ utility is poor (high median relative error, high total variation distance, low Spearman rank correlation), and at $\epsilon = 1.0$ or $\infty$ utility approaches or matches the non-private baseline. The best-epsilon selection ($\epsilon = 1.0$) reflects that on mini no $\epsilon$ reached the 80\% mean-preservation target, so $\epsilon = 1.0$ was chosen as the best available (highest mean utility preserved) while keeping a finite privacy guarantee. Organizations can tune $\epsilon$ downward for stronger privacy (accepting more error) or upward for higher accuracy (weaker privacy). The Laplace mechanism's advantage at low $\epsilon$ is consistent with its tighter tail for the same scale parameter in one dimension; the Analytic Gaussian is calibrated for $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP and yields a smaller $\sigma$ than the classical bound for the same $(\epsilon,\delta)$, which can help on vector-valued or multi-column outputs (Q7, Q8, Q9).

\subsection{Interpretation of the Privacy-Utility Tradeoff}
\label{sec:disc_interpretation}

We evaluated query utility across multiple privacy budgets using median relative error, total variation distance, and Spearman rank correlation. As expected, increasing $\epsilon$ improves utility because less noise is added to query results. In the \textbf{baseline} variant, utility continued improving until approximately $\epsilon = 1.0$, which produced the highest fraction of queries passing the predefined accuracy thresholds (median relative error $\leq 0.25$, total variation distance $\leq 0.15$, Spearman $\rho \geq 0.5$). As a result, $\epsilon = 1.0$ was identified as the optimal privacy-utility tradeoff for the baseline configuration and was used for full-database runs.

In the \textbf{advance} variant, the selected optimal $\epsilon$ shifted to \textbf{0.5} for both Laplace and Gaussian (from the same utility-preservation scoring on mini). This indicates that the advance implementation can achieve similar levels of query accuracy while requiring a smaller privacy budget: the advance approach preserves more privacy while maintaining comparable utility. The shift occurs because the utility curve in the advance runs reaches its plateau earlier, so that increasing $\epsilon$ beyond 0.5 yields only marginal improvements in accuracy.

An important observation is that even without privacy noise ($\epsilon = \infty$), the system achieves approximately 80\% query pass rate rather than 100\%. A subset of queries therefore fails the evaluation thresholds regardless of noise, likely due to inherent variability in query structure, small group sizes (e.g., on mini), or metric sensitivity. Consequently, the optimal privacy budget corresponds to the smallest $\epsilon$ that reaches the utility plateau rather than the largest possible $\epsilon$.

Overall, the advance variant improves the privacy-utility tradeoff by achieving comparable query accuracy at a lower privacy budget ($\epsilon = 0.5$) compared to the baseline configuration ($\epsilon = 1.0$). The baseline results reported in the main body of this report use $\epsilon = 1.0$; advance runs at $\epsilon = 0.5$ offer a stronger guarantee for deployment when that variant is used.

\subsection{Practical Implications}
\label{sec:disc_practical}

For Intel (or similar) telemetry, releasing the 12 query answers at $\epsilon = 1.0$ per query (or a composed total $\epsilon_{\text{total}} = 12$ under basic composition) provides a concrete deployment point: most queries achieve pass thresholds, and the guarantee is auditable from the published sensitivity and mechanism. Queries that remain challenging (Q4 vendor percentage, Q6 browser winner, Q12 ranked processes on mini) suggest either allocating more budget to those queries, using mechanism-specific tweaks (e.g., Report Noisy Max for Q6), or accepting lower utility for the most sensitive releases. The pipeline is reproducible (fixed seed, versioned scripts) and can be re-run on updated data or with different epsilon allocations.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:disc_limitations}

Limitations include the following. (1) \textbf{Mini vs full:} Mini results are noisier and more variable due to smaller group sizes (e.g., Q5 has 11 rows, Q12 has few users); full-database results at $\epsilon = 1.0$ are more stable. (2) \textbf{Metric coverage:} The evaluation summary does not include queries 10 and 11 in all runs; the report therefore emphasises queries 1 to 9 and 12. (3) \textbf{Single $\epsilon$ on full:} Full database was run only at the selected best $\epsilon$ (1.0), so privacy-utility curves on full data are not available unless additional runs are performed. (4) \textbf{Baseline vs advance:} Full-database results and figures in this report use the baseline variant at $\epsilon = 1.0$. The advance variant selects $\epsilon = 0.5$ on mini (Section~\ref{sec:disc_interpretation}); a full-database run of the advance variant at $\epsilon = 0.5$ could be compared in future work. (5) \textbf{Composition:} We use basic composition; advanced composition or RDP accounting could reduce the total $\epsilon$ for the same per-query budgets.

\subsection{Assumptions and Threat Model}
\label{sec:disc_threat}

We assume \emph{user-level} (device-level) differential privacy: the adversary cannot distinguish between datasets that differ in one GUID's entire contribution. We do not model collusion among multiple analysts or repeated adaptively chosen queries beyond the fixed 12; in practice, the same budget would be consumed each time the pipeline is run. We assume the reporting layer and clipping are applied correctly so that sensitivity bounds hold; errors in SQL clipping would invalidate the guarantee. The threat model is standard for DP query release: we protect against any single user's data being inferred from the released statistics, up to the $(\epsilon,\delta)$ parameters.

\subsection{Deployment Considerations}
\label{sec:disc_deployment}

Deployment would involve: (1) exporting baseline query results from the current database; (2) running the Laplace and Gaussian mechanisms at the chosen $\epsilon$ (e.g., 1.0) with a fixed seed for reproducibility; (3) storing the noisy outputs in a secure but accessible location for analysts; (4) documenting the composed $\epsilon_{\text{total}}$ and $\delta_{\text{total}}$ for transparency. Re-running the pipeline on a schedule would consume the same budget each run unless the data is considered a new dataset under a separate budget.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\subsection{Summary}
We designed and implemented a differentially private query-release system for Intel telemetry. The system answers 12 analytical queries by adding calibrated Laplace or Analytic Gaussian noise to query outputs, after bounding each device's contribution via clipping. Two implementation variants (baseline and advance) are available; they are mathematically identical, with the advance variant using precomputed scale caching for faster runs. Global sensitivity is derived from clipping constants and query structure ($\Delta = C/k_{\min}$ for averages, $\Delta = C$ for sums, $\Delta = 1$ for counts and conservative percentage columns). We evaluated both mechanisms on a mini (subsample) and full database across a range of $\epsilon$ values. All reported results and figures use the \textbf{baseline} variant. At strong privacy ($\epsilon = 0.01$), Laplace consistently matched or outperformed Gaussian on median relative error, total variation distance, and Spearman rank correlation. Best-epsilon selection on mini recommended $\epsilon = 1.0$ for the baseline; at $\epsilon = 1.0$, most queries pass their utility thresholds on the full database. The \textbf{advance} variant typically attains similar utility at $\epsilon = 0.5$, offering a stronger privacy guarantee for deployment. The pipeline is reproducible (fixed seed, versioned scripts) and provides an auditable $(\epsilon,\delta)$ guarantee under sequential composition.

\subsection{Impact}
The work demonstrates that query release under differential privacy is tractable at scale for telemetry analytics: sensitivity is bounded by design, mechanisms are standard and well understood, and the privacy-utility tradeoff can be tuned via $\epsilon$. It provides a rigorous, deployable alternative to releasing raw or lightly anonymized aggregates and avoids the pitfalls of synthetic data generation for this setting. The repository (export baseline, DP mechanisms, evaluation, and best-epsilon selection) can serve as a template for other telemetry or event-log systems where user-level DP is required.

\subsection{Future Work}
Future work includes: extension to the remaining benchmark queries (e.g., 10 and 11) in the evaluation pipeline; exploration of advanced composition and RDP accounting to tighten the composed guarantee; comparison with other mechanisms (e.g., the exponential mechanism for rankings); explicit Report Noisy Max for Q6 in the mechanism code and evaluation; and generation of privacy-utility curve figures from the per-query comparison CSVs for publication.
