\begin{abstract}
Telemetry from software and hardware systems is vital for product analytics and quality monitoring, but releasing aggregate statistics can expose individuals to re-identification even when identifiers are removed. We address this with a rigorous, practical approach: a differentially private query-release system that answers a fixed set of aggregate queries by adding calibrated noise to query outputs after bounding each device's contribution. We apply the Laplace and Analytic Gaussian mechanisms to 12 benchmark queries over Intel telemetry, covering battery usage, display and browser statistics, power and sleep metrics, and process rankings; a total privacy budget is allocated across queries. Evaluated on mini and full databases, Laplace matches or outperforms Gaussian at strong privacy, and at the selected $\epsilon$ most queries pass on the full database. The pipeline is reproducible and provides an auditable $(\epsilon,\delta)$ guarantee, supporting product and quality teams without access to raw user-level data.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Hardware and software vendors such as Intel rely on telemetry to understand how products behave in the field. They need aggregate statistics on battery usage, display and power metrics, adoption of features and configurations, and failure or performance patterns by geography, device type, and workload. These insights drive quality improvement, support prioritization, and product planning. To obtain them, vendors collect event logs and system attributes from participating devices: each record is associated with a device identifier (typically a GUID), and data is shipped to central systems for aggregation and analysis. The resulting datasets are large, high-dimensional, and user-level, which makes them valuable for analytics but also sensitive. Releasing aggregate statistics, even after stripping direct identifiers, can still enable re-identification or linkage when combined with other information. The tension between the need for actionable insights and the obligation to protect individuals is the problem we address.

In this work we apply \emph{differentially private query release} to Intel telemetry: we answer a fixed set of analytical queries by adding calibrated noise to their outputs after bounding each device's contribution via clipping, and we evaluate two standard mechanisms (Laplace and Analytic Gaussian) on mini and full databases.

\subsection{Motivation}
\label{sec:motivation}

Organizations that collect telemetry at scale need aggregate statistics for collaboration, benchmarking, and research while protecting individual users; removal of direct identifiers alone does not prevent inference from rich behavioral traces. Query-based release under Differential Privacy gives analysts noisy answers to predefined queries with a formal guarantee that the probability of any output changes by at most a factor $e^\epsilon$ (and an additive $\delta$ when applicable) when one user's data is added or removed. Implementing this in practice requires defining the unit of privacy (here, one GUID), deriving and bounding each query's global sensitivity, choosing a mechanism and calibrating noise to that sensitivity, and accounting for composition when multiple queries are answered. This work does that for a large telemetry corpus and a fixed set of 12 analytical queries. An alternative would be private synthetic data generation: learn a DP model of the full data distribution, generate a synthetic dataset with the same schema, and release it so that analysts can run arbitrary queries without further privacy cost. That approach offers flexibility and a one-time budget, but is less suited to our setting: our telemetry is large (order of terabytes), high-dimensional, and sparse, and many queries join dozens of columns (e.g., web category usage by persona, country, and CPU). Modelling the full joint distribution under DP is extremely demanding at that scale, and synthetic data tends either to over-smooth, to miss rare but important patterns, or to consume a large budget for the whole distribution instead of the summaries we need. Because we have a fixed set of 12 well-defined aggregate queries and an enterprise reporting use case, we adopt query release instead. We spend the privacy budget only on those 12 answers, bound sensitivity tightly per GUID and per query, calibrate noise to each metric type (counts, averages, histograms), and allocate epsilon per query by importance. That yields better accuracy for the statistics we actually use and keeps the guarantee auditable and the implementation tractable at scale.

\subsection{Differential Privacy}
\label{sec:dp_definitions}

Differential privacy (DP) is a mathematical definition of what it means for a randomized computation to limit the harm to an individual from participating in a dataset. The idea, due to Dwork and colleagues \cite{dwork2006calibrating,dwork2014promise}, is that the \emph{distribution} of the mechanism's output should change only slightly when one person's data is added or removed. Thus, an adversary who sees the output cannot confidently tell whether any one individual was present, so participation carries bounded privacy risk. Formally, a mechanism $\mathcal{M}$ is differentially private if for every pair of datasets that differ in one person's records, and for every subset $S$ of possible outputs, the probability $\Pr[\mathcal{M}(D) \in S]$ is close to $\Pr[\mathcal{M}(D') \in S]$. The standard way to quantify ``close'' is with two parameters: $\epsilon \geq 0$ (a multiplicative bound) and $\delta \geq 0$ (an additive slack). The central inequality is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dpdef_highlevel}
\Pr[\mathcal{M}(D) \in S] \;\leq\; e^{\epsilon} \cdot \Pr[\mathcal{M}(D') \in S] \;+\; \delta.
\end{equation}
When $\delta = 0$, the guarantee is \emph{pure} $\epsilon$-differential privacy: the output distribution changes by at most a factor $e^\epsilon$ when one person's data is removed. Smaller $\epsilon$ and $\delta$ mean stronger privacy; in practice $\epsilon$ is often chosen in a range from about 0.01 (very strong) to a few units (weaker but more accurate).

Any finite $\epsilon$ yields a formal $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP release; only $\epsilon = \infty$ corresponds to no noise. We now make the definition precise and state the sensitivity and mechanisms we use.

\paragraph{Neighboring datasets.}
We use \emph{user-level} (device-level) differential privacy. Two datasets $D$ and $D'$ are \emph{neighboring}, written $D \sim D'$, if one can be obtained from the other by adding or removing all records belonging to a single GUID. Thus the ``one person'' whose presence or absence we protect is one device's entire contribution to the dataset.

\paragraph{Formal definition.}
A randomized mechanism $\mathcal{M}$ with range $\mathcal{R}$ satisfies \emph{$(\epsilon,\delta)$-differential privacy} if for all neighboring $D$, $D'$ and all measurable $S \subseteq \mathcal{R}$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dpdef}
\Pr[\mathcal{M}(D) \in S] \leq e^{\epsilon} \, \Pr[\mathcal{M}(D') \in S] + \delta.
\end{equation}
When $\delta = 0$, we say $\mathcal{M}$ satisfies \emph{pure} $\epsilon$-DP \cite{dwork2014promise}.

\paragraph{Global sensitivity.}
To add the right amount of noise, we need a bound on how much the \emph{query function} $f$ can change when one person's data changes. The \emph{global sensitivity} of $f$ is the maximum change over all neighboring datasets. For a real-valued $f$,
\begin{equation}
\Delta f \;=\; \max_{D \sim D'} \, \bigl| f(D) - f(D') \bigr|.
\end{equation}
For a vector-valued $f$ with values in $\mathbb{R}^d$, we use two norms: the $\ell_1$ global sensitivity $\Delta_1 f = \max_{D \sim D'} \sum_{i=1}^d |f_i(D) - f_i(D')|$ (for the Laplace mechanism) and the $\ell_2$ global sensitivity $\Delta_2 f = \max_{D \sim D'} \|f(D) - f(D')\|_2$ (for the Gaussian mechanism). Sensitivity must be a worst-case, data-independent bound. For the query types in this work we use: \textbf{Count:} $\Delta f = 1$. \textbf{Sum with per-GUID clipping at $C$:} $\Delta f = C$. \textbf{Average} over at least $k_{\min}$ GUIDs with per-GUID contribution clipped to $C$: $\Delta f = C/k_{\min}$. \textbf{Histogram} (one bin per GUID): each GUID affects at most one bin by 1, so the $\ell_1$ sensitivity of the full vector is 1 and the $\ell_2$ sensitivity is 1.

\paragraph{Laplace mechanism.}
For a function $f$ with $\ell_1$ global sensitivity $\Delta_1 f$, the Laplace mechanism \cite{dwork2006calibrating,dwork2014promise} releases
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{M}(D) \;=\; f(D) + \text{Lap}(0, b), \qquad b \;=\; \frac{\Delta_1 f}{\epsilon}.
\end{equation}
Here $\text{Lap}(0,b)$ denotes a draw from the Laplace distribution with mean 0 and scale $b$ (so the variance is $2b^2$). This mechanism satisfies \emph{pure} $\epsilon$-DP with no $\delta$ term, making it particularly efficient for scalar aggregate queries. For vector-valued $f$, we use the $\ell_1$ sensitivity of the vector and add independent $\text{Lap}(0, \Delta_1 f/\epsilon)$ to each coordinate; the resulting mechanism is $\epsilon$-DP \cite{dwork2014promise}.

\paragraph{Gaussian and Analytic Gaussian Mechanism.}
For $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP, the Gaussian mechanism releases $f(D) + Z$ where $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)$. The classical analysis gives a sufficient condition $\sigma \geq (\Delta_2 f / \epsilon) \sqrt{2 \ln(1.25/\delta)}$, which is not tight. The \emph{Analytic Gaussian Mechanism} \cite{balle2018improving} calibrates $\sigma$ via the exact $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP condition. For a scalar output with $\ell_2$ sensitivity $\Delta$, the mechanism is $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP if and only if
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:analytic_gaussian}
\Phi\left( \frac{\Delta}{2\sigma} - \frac{\epsilon\sigma}{\Delta} \right) \;-\; e^\epsilon \cdot \Phi\left( -\frac{\Delta}{2\sigma} - \frac{\epsilon\sigma}{\Delta} \right) \;\leq\; \delta,
\end{equation}
where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF \cite{balle2018improving}. We find the smallest $\sigma$ satisfying this inequality (e.g., by binary search). For vector-valued $f$, we use the $\ell_2$ sensitivity $\Delta_2 f$ in place of $\Delta$. This analytic calibration yields a smaller $\sigma$ than the classical bound for the same $(\epsilon,\delta)$, hence better utility.

\paragraph{Composition and post-processing.}
If $\mathcal{M}_1$ is $(\epsilon_1,\delta_1)$-DP and $\mathcal{M}_2$ is $(\epsilon_2,\delta_2)$-DP, their sequential composition is $(\epsilon_1+\epsilon_2, \delta_1+\delta_2)$-DP (basic composition \cite{dwork2014promise}). We allocate a total budget $\epsilon_{\text{total}}$ across the 12 queries and state the composed guarantee explicitly (Section~\ref{sec:budget}). Post-processing does not weaken the guarantee: if $\mathcal{M}$ is $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP and $g$ is any deterministic function, then $g \circ \mathcal{M}$ is $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP. Hence clamping outputs to non-negative values or re-normalising percentages after adding noise costs no extra privacy.

\paragraph{Report Noisy Max.}
For queries that release the identity of the maximum over a set of counts (e.g., the most popular browser per country), we use Report Noisy Max \cite{dwork2014promise}: add independent $\text{Lap}(0, 1/\epsilon)$ to each count (each count has sensitivity 1), then release the index of the maximum noisy count. The whole selection is $\epsilon$-DP.

\subsection{Telemetry Data}
\label{sec:telemetry_data}

The data used in this work is Intel telemetry logs. The source data consists of 23 tables across two schemas. Each row is associated with a device identifier (GUID); multiple rows per GUID are common. The data exhibits heavy-tailed distributions; hence a small fraction of GUIDs can contribute disproportionately to aggregates. We therefore \emph{clip} per-GUID contributions before aggregation so that global sensitivity is bounded by a fixed constant. We cap quantities (including the number of power-on events per GUID and the total duration per GUID in a given category) at constants $C_1$, $C_2$, and similar bounds, as specified per query in Section~\ref{sec:methods}. These caps are chosen so that global sensitivity is bounded in the worst case (e.g., when a group has exactly $k_{\min}$ devices) while still covering typical or high-percentile usage (e.g., 600\,s for display duration, 720\,min for daily sleep time, 10\,W for per-user power). The aggregate query is then evaluated on the clipped view and the DP mechanism is applied to the query output. Clipping is a deterministic pre-processing step; global sensitivity is computed with respect to the clipped query function only.

We use a specialized reporting layer of 22 pre-aggregated tables built from the raw tables via a SQL build script, and we run 12 benchmark queries covering battery usage by geography and CPU family, display device vendor market share, browser popularity by country, OS-level MODS blockers, RAM utilization, persona-level web category usage, and process power rankings. The 12 query results are exported as baseline (non-private) CSVs. We then apply both the Laplace and the Analytic Gaussian Mechanism to every query (Section~\ref{sec:global_model}), with epsilon allocated via sequential composition (Section~\ref{sec:budget}). Post-processing consists of clamping numeric outputs to non-negative values where required and re-normalising percentage columns to sum to 100\% where applicable. The pipeline is developed and validated on a subsample database (approximately 5\,GB) before execution on the full dataset.

\subsection{Applications}
\label{sec:applications}

Privacy-preserving query release over telemetry supports usage metrics and feature adoption by segment, performance and health monitoring by geography or hardware family, market share and ranking statistics, and anomaly detection (identification of segments with unusually high error rates or power draw). Analysts receive noisy answers suitable for trend analysis and prioritisation without access to raw user-level data. The same framework applies to other telemetry or event-log datasets where user-level differential privacy is required.

\subsection{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Differential privacy was introduced in \cite{dwork2006calibrating} and is treated in depth in \cite{dwork2014promise}, which covers the Laplace and Gaussian mechanisms, global sensitivity, and composition. The Analytic Gaussian Mechanism of \cite{balle2018improving} improves noise calibration for $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP. Query answering under DP in database and analytics settings requires bounding sensitivity (typically via clipping) and allocating privacy budget across queries; our guarantees follow from these standard tools and explicit sequential composition.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

This section describes how we obtain the data, how the pipeline is structured, the global privacy model (unit of privacy, sensitivity, mechanisms, composition), the choice and meaning of the three evaluation metrics (median relative error, total variation distance, and Spearman rank correlation) and their assignment to queries, the allocation of the privacy budget across queries, and the use of multiple epsilon values to trace the privacy-utility curve. We then give the full mathematical definition, sensitivity, mechanism, and evaluation metric for each of the 12 queries (Sections~\ref{sec:q1} to \ref{sec:q12}).

\subsection{Data Access and Reporting Layer}
\label{sec:data_access}

Raw Intel telemetry is stored in 23 tables across two schemas. We build two DuckDB databases: a subsample with capped table sizes for development, and a full database for production. A build step (SQL script \texttt{00\_build\_reporting\_tables.sql}) constructs 22 reporting tables that resolve joins and normalise column names. The 12 benchmark queries are executed against this reporting layer; their results are exported and serve as the non-private baseline. Per-query clipping is applied in SQL so that each device's contribution is bounded before aggregation; global sensitivity is derived from these clipping constants and the query structure only.

\subsection{Project and Pipeline Structure}
\label{sec:project_structure}

The pipeline has six stages. Raw telemetry is loaded into DuckDB and a build step produces 22 reporting tables. The 12 benchmark queries are then run with per-GUID clipping in SQL to obtain the non-private baseline. For the DP release we run the Laplace and Analytic Gaussian mechanism at each $\epsilon$ in $\{0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.5, 1.0, \infty\}$ (with $\infty$ as the no-noise reference); two implementation variants are defined in Section~\ref{sec:mechanism_variants}. Utility is quantified by median relative error, total variation distance, and Spearman rank correlation (Section~\ref{sec:eval_metrics_methods}). Evaluation computes per-query statistics and Laplace vs Gaussian comparison across $\epsilon$. Best-epsilon selection uses a utility-preservation score relative to the $\epsilon = \infty$ baseline: Tier 1 is the smallest $\epsilon$ where mean utility preserved reaches 80\%, with a fallback to the $\epsilon$ with highest score otherwise. Finally, privacy-utility curves, pass rate, and Pareto frontier are produced from the evaluation outputs.

\subsection{Mechanism Variants: Baseline and Advance}
\label{sec:mechanism_variants}

The pipeline implements each mechanism in two variants: \textbf{baseline} and \textbf{advance}. They are \emph{mathematically identical} (same noise distribution and privacy guarantee); the only difference is how the noise scale is computed at run time.

In the \textbf{baseline} variant the scale is recomputed for every (query, column, $\epsilon$) triple: $b = \Delta_1 f / \epsilon$ for Laplace, and $\sigma$ via binary search for the Analytic Gaussian \cite{balle2018improving}. In the \textbf{advance} variant a \emph{scale cache} is built once per (sensitivity, $\epsilon$) pair and looked up during release; this reduces runtime (often 2--4$\times$ faster) without changing outputs or guarantees. All results and figures in this report use the baseline variant; the advance variant typically attains similar utility at a lower $\epsilon$ (see Section~\ref{sec:disc_interpretation}).

\subsection{Global Privacy Model}
\label{sec:global_model}

\textbf{Unit of privacy.} We use \emph{user-level} differential privacy: the unit of privacy is one GUID. Neighboring datasets $D$ and $D'$ differ in all rows associated with exactly one GUID (i.e., adding or removing one user's entire activity).

\textbf{Contribution bounding.} Before aggregation we apply per-GUID contribution clipping so that the influence of any single GUID on the query output is bounded. Clipping is a deterministic, data-independent pre-processing step; global sensitivity is computed with respect to the clipped query function.

\textbf{Sensitivity.} For each query we derive the global sensitivity $\Delta f$ (or $\Delta_1 f$, $\Delta_2 f$ for vector outputs) from the clipping bounds and the query structure.

\textbf{Mechanisms.} We run \emph{both} the Laplace and the Analytic Gaussian Mechanism on every query so that we can compare their privacy-utility tradeoff in the evaluation. The per-query ``assignment'' in the table and subsections indicates which mechanism is \emph{preferred} for that query type (because it typically yields better utility for the same privacy budget), not which one we run. Laplace is preferred for count queries, histograms, Report Noisy Max, and low-dimensional aggregates. The Analytic Gaussian Mechanism is preferred for high-dimensional or multi-column outputs (Queries 7, 8, 9) because its analytic calibration yields a smaller $\sigma$ than the classical bound for the same $(\epsilon,\delta)$, improving utility when the budget is spread over many dimensions. Specifically:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Laplace mechanism} (pure $\epsilon$-DP): release $f(D) + \text{Lap}(0, b)$ with $b = \Delta_1 f / \epsilon$.
    \item \textbf{Analytic Gaussian Mechanism} ($(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP): release $f(D) + \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ where $\sigma$ is calibrated via \cite{balle2018improving}. We fix $\delta = 10^{-6}$ per query.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Composition.} The 12 queries are answered under \emph{sequential composition}: if query $i$ uses $(\epsilon_i, \delta_i)$-DP, the total guarantee is $(\sum_{i=1}^{12} \epsilon_i,\, \sum_{i=1}^{12} \delta_i)$-DP. We allocate a total budget $\epsilon_{\text{total}}$ so that $\sum_{i=1}^{12} \epsilon_i \leq \epsilon_{\text{total}}$. When a single query releases multiple numeric outputs, we either split that query's $\epsilon_i$ across outputs or treat the output as a vector and apply the vector-valued mechanism once.

\textbf{Post-processing.} Deterministic post-processing (clamping to non-negative, re-normalising percentages to sum to 100\%) does not consume privacy budget and preserves $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP.

\subsection{Query 1: Battery Power On Geographic Summary}
\label{sec:q1}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_batt\_dc\_events (guid, num\_power\_ons, duration\_mins); reporting.system\_sysinfo\_unique\_normalized (guid, countryname\_normalized).

\textbf{Research question.} Which countries have abnormally long battery discharge durations?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} Let $D$ be the dataset of per-GUID battery discharge events. For each country $c$, define the clipped per-GUID average of duration with $C = 60$ minutes:
\[
x_g^c = \text{clip}(\text{avg}_{\text{session}}(\text{duration\_mins}_g), C), \qquad
f(D)_c = \frac{1}{n_c} \sum_{g \in c} x_g^c, \quad n_c = |\{g : \text{country}(g) = c\}|,
\]
restricted to groups with $n_c \geq k_{\min} = 100$. Output: a vector of (country, avg\_duration) pairs, ordered by avg\_duration.

\textbf{Contribution bounding.} For each GUID: clip total \texttt{num\_power\_ons} to $C_1 = 10$ and total \texttt{duration\_mins} to $C_2 = 60$. Averages use $k_{\min} = 100$.

\textbf{Sensitivity.} Adding or removing one GUID changes $f(D)_c$ by at most $C/n_c$. Worst case over neighboring datasets is $n_c = k_{\min}$, so \emph{global} sensitivity is $\Delta = C/k_{\min} = 60/100 = 0.60$ (we use only this; no data-dependent reduction). Per country we release one scalar (avg\_duration); Laplace scale $b = \Delta/\epsilon_1$.

\textbf{Mechanism:} Laplace: add $\text{Lap}(0, \Delta/\epsilon_1)$ to avg\_duration per country. Single draw per output. Epsilon: $\epsilon_1$ (medium).

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Median relative error (on avg\_duration).

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$; ranking and z-scores are deterministic functions of the noisy output.

\subsection{Query 2: Battery On Duration by CPU Family and Generation}
\label{sec:q2}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_cpu\_metadata (guid, marketcodename, cpugen); reporting.system\_batt\_dc\_events (guid, duration\_mins).

\textbf{Research question.} Which CPU family-generation combinations have abnormal battery duration?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} For each group $c = (\text{marketcodename}, \text{cpugen})$ with $C = 60$:
\[
x_g^c = \text{clip}(\text{avg}_{\text{event}}(\text{duration\_mins}_g), C), \qquad
f(D)_c = \frac{1}{n_c} \sum_{g \in c} x_g^c, \quad n_c \geq k_{\min} = 100.
\]
Output ordered by avg\_duration.

\textbf{Contribution bounding.} Clip each GUID's total \texttt{duration\_mins} to $C = 60$.

\textbf{Sensitivity.} One GUID changes the group average by at most $C/n_c$; worst case $n_c = k_{\min}$ gives $\Delta = C/k_{\min} = 60/100 = 0.60$. Laplace scale $b = \Delta/\epsilon_2$.

\textbf{Mechanism:} Laplace: add $\text{Lap}(0, \Delta/\epsilon_2)$ to avg\_duration per group. Single draw. Epsilon: $\epsilon_2$ (medium).

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Median relative error (on avg\_duration).

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$.

\subsection{Query 3: Display Devices Connection Type Resolution Durations AC/DC}
\label{sec:q3}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_display\_devices (guid, connection\_type, resolution\_height, resolution\_width, duration\_ac, duration\_dc).

\textbf{Research question.} Which display connection-type and resolution combinations have the longest AC usage time?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} For each group $c = (\text{connection\_type}, \text{resolution})$ with $C = 600$ seconds (10 minutes):
\[
x_g^c = \text{clip}(\text{avg}_{\text{session}}(\text{duration\_ac}_g), C), \qquad
f(D)_c = \frac{1}{n_c} \sum_{g \in c} x_g^c, \quad n_c \geq k_{\min} = 50.
\]
Output ranked by $f(D)_c$ descending.

\textbf{Contribution bounding.} Clip per-GUID \texttt{duration\_ac} and \texttt{duration\_dc} to $C = 600$ seconds.

\textbf{Sensitivity.} One GUID changes the group average by at most $C/n_c$; worst case $n_c = k_{\min}$ gives $\Delta = C/k_{\min} = 600/50 = 12.0$ seconds. Laplace scale $b = \Delta/\epsilon_3$ for the released average\_duration\_on\_ac.

\textbf{Mechanism:} Laplace: add $\text{Lap}(0, \Delta/\epsilon_3)$ to average\_duration\_on\_ac per group. Single draw. Epsilon: $\epsilon_3$ (smaller).

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Spearman $\rho$ (rank correlation on average\_duration\_on\_ac).

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$; ranking is deterministic post-processing.

\subsection{Query 4: Display Devices Vendors Percentage}
\label{sec:q4}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_display\_devices (guid, vendor\_name).

\textbf{Research question.} What fraction of systems use each display vendor?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} Let $N = \sum_v n_v$ be the total number of distinct GUIDs. For each vendor $v$:
\[
n_v = |\{g : \text{vendor}(g) = v\}|, \qquad f(D)_v = (n_v / N) \times 100 \text{ (percentage of systems)}.
\]
Output: vector (vendor, percentage) summing to 100\%.

\textbf{Contribution bounding.} One GUID contributes to exactly one vendor. We add noise \emph{directly to the percentage column} (the baseline export already contains percentages computed from counts). The true sensitivity of a percentage to one GUID is $O(1/N)$; we use a deliberate conservative choice $\Delta = 1.0$ (one percentage point on the 0--100 scale) so that the guarantee holds regardless of $N$. This choice drives higher median relative error (e.g., 0.66 on full) and could be relaxed in future work (e.g., noising counts then forming percentages).

\textbf{Sensitivity.} $\Delta = 1.0$. Laplace scale $b = \Delta/\epsilon_4$ per vendor percentage.

\textbf{Mechanism:} Laplace: add $\text{Lap}(0, \Delta/\epsilon_4)$ to each vendor's percentage. Single draw. Epsilon: $\epsilon_4$ (smaller).

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Median relative error (on percentage\_of\_systems).

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$; re-normalise to sum to 100\% (deterministic).

\subsection{Query 5: MODS Blockers by OS Name and Codename}
\label{sec:q5}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_mods\_top\_blocker\_hist (guid, dt);
reporting.system\_os\_codename\_history (guid, min\_ts, max\_ts, os\_name, os\_codename).
Join on GUID and time windows.

\textbf{Research question.} Which OS versions generate the most MODS blocker events per system?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} For each group $c = (\text{os\_name}, \text{os\_codename})$ with $C = 50$:
\[
e_g^c = \text{clip}(\text{COUNT}(\text{events for } g \in c), C),
\]
\[
f(D)_c = \frac{1}{n_c} \sum_{g \in c} e_g^c \text{ (avg.\ clipped entries per system)}, \quad n_c \geq k_{\min} = 10.
\]

\textbf{Contribution bounding.} Clip each GUID's entry count to $C = 50$ per group.

\textbf{Sensitivity.} One GUID changes the group average by at most $C/n_c$; worst case $n_c = k_{\min}$ gives $\Delta = C/k_{\min} = 50/10 = 5.0$. Laplace scale $b = \Delta/\epsilon_5$.

\textbf{Mechanism:} Laplace: add $\text{Lap}(0, \Delta/\epsilon_5)$ to entries\_per\_system per group. Single draw. Epsilon: $\epsilon_5$ (smaller).

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Median relative error (on entries\_per\_system).

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$.

\subsection{Query 6: Most Popular Browser in Each Country by System Count}
\label{sec:q6}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_sysinfo\_unique\_normalized (guid, countryname\_normalized); reporting.system\_web\_cat\_usage (guid, browser).

\textbf{Research question.} Which browser is most used in each country?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} For each country $c$ and browser $b$:
\[
\text{count}(c,b) = |\{g : \text{country}(g)=c,\, \text{browser}(g)=b\}|, \qquad f(D)_c = \arg\max_b \text{count}(c,b).
\]
Output: one (country, browser) pair per country.

\textbf{Sensitivity.} Each $\text{count}(c,b)$ has global sensitivity $\Delta = 1$ (one GUID changes one count by 1).

\textbf{Mechanism:} \textbf{Report Noisy Max.} Per country: add $\text{Lap}(0, 1/\epsilon_6)$ to each browser count; release $\arg\max_b (\text{count}(c,b) + \text{noise}_b)$. This satisfies $\epsilon_6$-DP per country. Both the ``Laplace'' and ``Gaussian'' pipeline runs use this same Report Noisy Max (Laplace noise on the count); the two rows for Q6 in the evaluation table are the two runs, not two different mechanisms.

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Spearman $\rho$ (rank correlation on winner ordering per country).

\textbf{Post-processing:} None beyond argmax.

\subsection{Query 7: On/Off MODS Sleep Summary by CPU Market Codename and Generation}
\label{sec:q7}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_on\_off\_suspend\_time\_day (guid, on\_time, off\_time, mods\_time, sleep\_time); reporting.system\_cpu\_metadata (guid, marketcodename, cpugen).

\textbf{Research question.} Which CPU families spend an abnormally high fraction of time in Modern Standby (MODS)?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} For each group $c = (\text{marketcodename}, \text{cpugen})$ with $C = 720$ minutes per day (12 hours):
\[
x_g^c = \text{clip}(\text{avg}_{\text{day}}(\text{mods\_time}_g), C), \qquad
f(D)_c = \frac{1}{n_c} \sum_{g \in c} x_g^c \text{ (avg\_modern\_sleep\_time)}, \quad n_c \geq k_{\min} = 100.
\]
Output includes multiple time columns; the evaluation metric uses only avg\_modern\_sleep\_time.

\textbf{Contribution bounding.} Clip per-GUID total time per category to $C = 720$ minutes per day. For the key column (avg\_modern\_sleep\_time): $\Delta = C/k_{\min} = 720/100 = 7.2$ minutes. We add noise to \emph{every} released numeric column (or treat the release as one vector with joint L1/L2 sensitivity and one mechanism call).

\textbf{Sensitivity.} $\Delta = 7.2$ for the average; if releasing multiple columns, joint sensitivity is used (e.g., L2 over the vector of sensitivities).

\textbf{Mechanism:} Laplace or Analytic Gaussian Mechanism: add noise with scale $\Delta/\epsilon_7$ (Laplace) or $\sigma(\Delta,\epsilon_7,\delta)$ (Gaussian) to the released statistics. Single draw. Epsilon: $\epsilon_7$ (medium).

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Total variation distance (mean over sleep percentage columns).

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$; re-normalise percentages if computed.

\subsection{Query 8: Persona Web Category Usage Analysis}
\label{sec:q8}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_sysinfo\_unique\_normalized (guid, persona);
\newline
reporting.system\_web\_cat\_pivot\_duration (guid, category duration columns).

\textbf{Research question.} How does web browsing time distribute across 28 content categories for each user persona?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} For each persona $p$ and category $k$: $C_{\text{days}} = 365$, $C_{\text{dur}} = 3{,}600{,}000$ ms;
\[
\text{days}_g = \text{clip}(\text{COUNT}(\text{days for } g), C_{\text{days}}), \quad
\text{cat}_g^k = \text{clip}(\text{SUM}(\text{category}_k \text{ duration for } g), C_{\text{dur}}),
\]
\[
f(D)_{p,k} = \sum_{g \in p} \text{days}_g \cdot (\text{cat}_g^k / \text{total}_g) \big/ \sum_{g \in p} \text{days}_g.
\]
Output: 28-dimensional (fraction or percentage) vector per persona, summing to $\sim$100\%.

\textbf{Contribution bounding.} Per GUID, each category duration is clipped and $\text{total}_g$ is the sum of all category durations; hence $\text{cat}_g^k / \text{total}_g \in [0,1]$ by construction ($\text{total}_g \geq \text{cat}_g^k$). Thus each category output has sensitivity $\Delta \leq 1$ per column in normalised form. We use the \textbf{split-budget} approach: allocate $\epsilon_{\text{per column}} = \epsilon_8/28$ per column so the total composition is $\epsilon_8$. Both Laplace and Analytic Gaussian are implemented with this split (not the single vector release with $\ell_2$ sensitivity $\sqrt{28}$).

\textbf{Sensitivity.} Per column: $\Delta_j \leq 1$, $\epsilon_j = \epsilon_8/28$.

\textbf{Mechanism:} Laplace or Analytic Gaussian with $\epsilon_8/28$ per column. Single draw per output per column.

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Total variation distance (mean across persona category distributions).

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$; re-normalise each persona's row to sum to 100\%; optional Dirichlet smoothing to avoid zeros in KL.

\subsection{Query 9: Package Power by Country}
\label{sec:q9}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_sysinfo\_unique\_normalized (guid, countryname\_normalized); reporting.system\_hw\_pkg\_power (guid, nrs, mean).

\textbf{Research question.} Which countries have abnormally high CPU package power consumption?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} For each country $c$ with $C = 50$ W:
\[
x_g^c = \text{clip}(\text{SUM}(\text{nrs} \cdot \text{mean})/\text{SUM}(\text{nrs}) \text{ for } g, C), \qquad
f(D)_c = \frac{1}{n_c} \sum_{g \in c} x_g^c, \quad n_c \geq k_{\min} = 100.
\]

\textbf{Contribution bounding.} Clip per-GUID average power to $C = 50$ W.

\textbf{Sensitivity.} One GUID changes the group average by at most $C/n_c$; worst case $n_c = k_{\min}$ gives $\Delta = C/k_{\min} = 50/100 = 0.50$ W. Laplace scale $b = \Delta/\epsilon_9$.

\textbf{Mechanism:} Laplace or Analytic Gaussian Mechanism: add noise with scale $\Delta/\epsilon_9$. Single draw. Epsilon: $\epsilon_9$ (medium).

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Median relative error (on avg\_pkg\_power\_consumed).

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$.

\subsection{Query 10: Popular Browsers by Count Usage Percentage}
\label{sec:q10}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_web\_cat\_usage (guid, browser, duration\_ms).

\textbf{Research question.} What are the global market shares of browsers by system count (and optionally instance count, duration)?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} For each browser $b$:
\[
n_b = |\{g : \text{browser}(g) = b\}|, \quad N = \sum_b n_b, \qquad f(D)_b = (n_b / N) \times 100 \text{ (percent\_systems)}.
\]
Noise is added directly to the percentage columns (same conservative $\Delta = 1.0$ as for Q4).

\textbf{Sensitivity.} $\Delta = 1.0$ (percentage point). Laplace scale $b = \Delta/\epsilon_{10}$.

\textbf{Mechanism:} Laplace: add $\text{Lap}(0, \Delta/\epsilon_{10})$ to percent\_systems. Single draw. Epsilon: $\epsilon_{10}$ (smaller).

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Total variation distance (on percent\_systems distribution).

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$; re-normalise to sum to 100\%.

\subsection{Query 11: RAM Utilization Histogram}
\label{sec:q11}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_memory\_utilization (guid, sysinfo\_ram, nrs, avg\_percentage\_used).

\textbf{Research question.} How does average RAM utilisation distribute across RAM size bins?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} For each RAM bin $b$ (e.g., ram\_gb value) with $C = 100\%$:
\[
x_g^b = \text{clip}(\text{avg}_{\text{measurement}}(\text{pct\_used}_g), C), \qquad
f(D)_b = \frac{1}{n_b} \sum_{g \in b} x_g^b, \quad n_b \geq k_{\min} = 50.
\]
Output: (ram\_gb, avg\_percentage\_used) per bin.

\textbf{Contribution bounding.} Each GUID contributes to one bin; per-GUID avg percentage is in $[0,100]$, clipped for robustness.

\textbf{Sensitivity.} One GUID changes one bin's average by at most $C/n_b$; worst case $n_b = k_{\min}$ gives $\Delta = C/k_{\min} = 100/50 = 2.0$ percentage points. Laplace scale $b = \Delta/\epsilon_{11}$ per bin.

\textbf{Mechanism:} Laplace: add $\text{Lap}(0, \Delta/\epsilon_{11})$ to avg\_percentage\_used per bin. Single draw. Epsilon: $\epsilon_{11}$ (smaller).

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Total variation distance (on avg\_percentage\_used histogram, normalised to a distribution).

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$ (bins are independent; no re-normalisation required if releasing means).

\subsection{Query 12: Ranked Process Classifications}
\label{sec:q12}

\textbf{Source tables:} reporting.system\_mods\_power\_consumption (user\_id, total\_power\_consumption).

\textbf{Research question.} Which user IDs consume the most total CPU power across all processes?

\textbf{Non-private mathematical expression.} For each user $u$ with $C = 10$ (watts):
\[
f(D)_u = \text{clip}\Bigl(\sum_{\text{process}} \text{power\_consumption}_{u,\text{process}}, C\Bigr).
\]
Output: ranked list of (user\_id, total\_power\_consumption) by $f(D)_u$ descending. This is a \emph{SUM} query (not an average), so one user's removal changes that user's output by at most $C$.

\textbf{Sensitivity.} $\Delta = C = 10$ per user (global sensitivity of the sum). Laplace scale $b = \Delta/\epsilon_{12}$ per user. Composition: one vector release over all users; total $\epsilon_{12}$ for the query.

\textbf{Mechanism:} Laplace: add $\text{Lap}(0, \Delta/\epsilon_{12})$ to total\_power\_consumption per user. Single draw. Epsilon: $\epsilon_{12}$ (moderate).

\textbf{Evaluation metric:} Spearman $\rho$ (rank correlation on total\_power\_consumption). On the mini database, $\rho = 0$ across $\epsilon$ is expected: $\Delta = 10$ gives Laplace scale $b = 10/\epsilon$, so at $\epsilon = 1.0$ we have $b = 10$; this is large relative to the spread of total\_power\_consumption values (in $[0,100]$), so the ranking is heavily perturbed.

\textbf{Post-processing:} Clamp $\geq 0$; ranking is deterministic post-processing.

\subsection{Privacy Budget Allocation}
\label{sec:budget}

We fix a total privacy budget $\epsilon_{\text{total}}$ and allocate per-query budgets $\epsilon_1, \ldots, \epsilon_{12}$ such that
\[
\sum_{i=1}^{12} \epsilon_i \leq \epsilon_{\text{total}}.
\]
The allocation reflects the sensitivity and dimensionality of each query: Query 8 (28-dimensional) receives a larger share (about 20\% of $\epsilon_{\text{total}}$), with $\epsilon_8$ consumed by splitting $\epsilon_8/28$ per category column (Section~\ref{sec:q8}). Core usage queries Q1, Q2, Q7, Q9 receive a medium allocation; simple histograms and percentages (Q4, Q5, Q10, Q11) receive a smaller allocation; ranking and Report Noisy Max (Q6, Q12) receive a moderate allocation; Q3 receives a smaller allocation. For the Analytic Gaussian Mechanism we fix $\delta = 10^{-6}$ per query. Under sequential composition the full system satisfies $(\epsilon_{\text{total}}, \delta_{\text{total}})$-DP with $\epsilon_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^{12} \epsilon_i$ and $\delta_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^{12} \delta_i = 12 \times 10^{-6}$.

\subsection{Multiple Epsilon Evaluation and the Privacy-Utility Curve}
\label{sec:multi_epsilon}

We run at $\epsilon \in \{0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.5, 1.0, \infty\}$ to trace the privacy-utility curve and select an operating point for full-database runs (Section~\ref{sec:project_structure}). At each $\epsilon$ we compute the evaluation metrics and compare Laplace vs Gaussian; $\epsilon = \infty$ is the no-noise reference.

\subsection{Laplace vs Gaussian Comparison}
\label{sec:laplace_vs_gaussian}

The Laplace mechanism satisfies \emph{pure} $\epsilon$-DP and typically introduces less variance for scalar aggregate queries when $\epsilon$ is small. Because the Laplace noise scale is $b = \Delta/\epsilon$, at very small $\epsilon$ the injected noise is large but the Laplace distribution has lighter tails relative to the Gaussian for the same scale. The Analytic Gaussian Mechanism must maintain a probabilistic privacy bound parameterized by both $\epsilon$ and $\delta$, and to do so it calibrates $\sigma$ via the condition in Equation~\eqref{eq:analytic_gaussian}. At small $\epsilon$ this yields a larger effective noise standard deviation than Laplace for scalar outputs, which is why Laplace consistently outperforms Gaussian on one-dimensional aggregate queries at low $\epsilon$. For multi-dimensional outputs (e.g., Q7, Q8), the Analytic Gaussian Mechanism uses the $\ell_2$ sensitivity of the full vector and benefits from the geometry of high-dimensional noise, which is why it is preferred for those queries.

Table~\ref{tab:lap_vs_gauss} summarises the comparison at $\epsilon = 0.01$ and $\epsilon = 1.0$ on the mini database. At strong privacy ($\epsilon = 0.01$), Laplace consistently matches or outperforms Gaussian: for median-relative-error queries the median relative error is lower under Laplace (e.g., Q1: 0.85 vs 1.0; Q5: 6.76 vs 18.16), and for Spearman-rank-correlation queries Laplace gives higher correlation (e.g., Q3: 0.47 vs 0.20). For total variation distance (Q7, Q8, Q10, Q11), Laplace again yields lower total variation distance at $\epsilon = 0.01$. At $\epsilon = 1.0$, both mechanisms improve substantially because the noise scale shrinks in proportion to $\epsilon$; Laplace remains slightly better on most scalar queries.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Privacy and Utility Tradeoff}
\label{sec:disc_tradeoff}

The results confirm the expected tradeoff: at $\epsilon = 0.01$ the noise scale $b = \Delta/\epsilon$ is large and error metrics are higher; at $\epsilon = 1.0$ or $\infty$ utility approaches the non-private baseline. Every finite $\epsilon$ is a formally differentially private release. Best-epsilon selection gave $\epsilon = 1.0$ for the baseline (Section~\ref{sec:results}); the Laplace mechanism's advantage at low $\epsilon$ is consistent with its pure $\epsilon$-DP calibration for scalar queries, while the Analytic Gaussian benefits multi-dimensional outputs (Q7, Q8, Q9) via $\ell_2$-sensitivity geometry.

\subsection{Interpretation of the Privacy-Utility Tradeoff}
\label{sec:disc_interpretation}

In the \textbf{baseline} variant, $\epsilon = 1.0$ was used for full-database runs (Section~\ref{sec:eval_metrics_methods}). In the \textbf{advance} variant the optimal $\epsilon$ is 0.5: the utility curve reaches its plateau earlier, so comparable accuracy is achieved at a stronger privacy guarantee. Even at $\epsilon = \infty$ the system achieves approximately 80\% pass rate; some queries miss the threshold due to query structure or small group sizes. Table~\ref{tab:eval_summary_advance} in Appendix D gives the advance variant evaluation summary.

\subsection{Comparison with Alternative Approach}
\label{sec:disc_comparison}

A parallel project group approached the same telemetry privacy problem via \emph{dataset modification}: rather than adding noise to query outputs after the fact, their pipeline perturbs or suppresses records at the data layer before any queries are run, aiming to bound the information leakage of the released dataset itself. This contrasts with our \emph{query-release} framework in several important respects. Our approach adds calibrated noise only to the 12 predefined query outputs, spending the privacy budget precisely on those statistics. This means analysts receive highly accurate private answers to exactly the queries we care about, while the sensitivity of each answer is bounded tightly query by query. The dataset-modification approach, on the other hand, releases a modified dataset from which analysts can run arbitrary queries, offering considerably more flexibility for future analysis. The tradeoff is that the privacy budget must be allocated against the entire joint distribution rather than just the targeted summaries, which either requires a larger total budget or accepts higher noise on any individual statistic. For our use case (a fixed set of 12 enterprise analytics queries over large, sparse telemetry), the query-release framework is more economical and auditable. The dataset-modification approach would be preferable if the downstream query workload were unknown at release time, or if a one-time release enabling ad-hoc analysis were the primary requirement. The two approaches thus occupy complementary points in the privacy-preserving analytics design space.

\subsection{Practical Implications}
\label{sec:disc_practical}

For Intel (or similar) telemetry, releasing the 12 query answers at $\epsilon = 1.0$ per query (or a composed total $\epsilon_{\text{total}} = 12$ under basic composition) provides a concrete deployment point: most queries achieve pass thresholds, and the guarantee is auditable from the published sensitivity and mechanism. Queries such as Q4 (vendor percentage), Q6 (browser winner), and Q12 (ranked processes on mini) can be improved further by allocating more budget, using mechanism-specific tweaks (e.g., Report Noisy Max for Q6), or tuning for those releases. The pipeline is reproducible (fixed seed, versioned scripts) and can be re-run on updated data or with different epsilon allocations.

\subsection{Scope and Future Directions}
\label{sec:disc_limitations}

The current implementation leaves room for extension. Mini results show greater variability due to smaller group sizes (e.g., Q5 has 11 rows, Q12 has few users); full-database results at $\epsilon = 1.0$ are more stable. The report emphasises queries 1 to 9 and 12; queries 10 and 11 can be added to the evaluation in future runs. Privacy-utility curves on full data can be obtained by running additional $\epsilon$ sweeps. Full-database results here use the baseline variant at $\epsilon = 1.0$; the advance variant selects $\epsilon = 0.5$ on mini (Section~\ref{sec:disc_interpretation}), and a full-database run at $\epsilon = 0.5$ for the advance variant would further validate the improved guarantee. We use \emph{basic composition}; advanced composition (e.g., RDP accounting) could tighten the composed $\epsilon$ for the same per-query budgets. Clipping bounds are set from domain knowledge and percentile inspection; data-adaptive or privately-selected clipping could improve utility further while preserving the DP guarantee.

\subsection{Assumptions and Threat Model}
\label{sec:disc_threat}

We assume \emph{user-level} (device-level) differential privacy: the adversary cannot distinguish between datasets that differ in one GUID's entire contribution. We do not model collusion among multiple analysts or repeated adaptively chosen queries beyond the fixed 12; in practice, the same budget would be consumed each time the pipeline is run. We assume the reporting layer and clipping are applied correctly so that sensitivity bounds hold; errors in SQL clipping would invalidate the guarantee. The threat model is standard for DP query release: we protect against any single user's data being inferred from the released statistics, up to the $(\epsilon,\delta)$ parameters.

\subsection{Deployment Considerations}
\label{sec:disc_deployment}

Deployment would involve: (1) exporting baseline query results from the current database; (2) running the Laplace and Gaussian mechanisms at the chosen $\epsilon$ (e.g., 1.0) with a fixed seed for reproducibility; (3) storing the noisy outputs in a secure but accessible location for analysts; (4) documenting the composed $\epsilon_{\text{total}}$ and $\delta_{\text{total}}$ for transparency. Re-running the pipeline on a schedule would consume the same budget each run unless the data is considered a new dataset under a separate budget.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\subsection{Summary}
We designed and implemented a differentially private query-release system for Intel telemetry: a fixed set of analytical queries is answered with calibrated Laplace or Analytic Gaussian noise after per-device clipping. We evaluated on mini and full databases; all reported results use the baseline variant (Section~\ref{sec:mechanism_variants}). Best-epsilon selection gave $\epsilon = 1.0$ for the baseline; at that value most queries pass on the full database. The advance variant attains similar utility at $\epsilon = 0.5$. The pipeline is reproducible and provides an auditable $(\epsilon,\delta)$ guarantee under sequential composition.

\subsection{Impact}
The work demonstrates that query release under differential privacy is tractable at scale for telemetry analytics: sensitivity is bounded by design, mechanisms are standard and well understood, and the privacy-utility tradeoff can be tuned via $\epsilon$. It provides a rigorous, deployable alternative to releasing raw or lightly anonymized aggregates and avoids the pitfalls of synthetic data generation for this setting. The repository (export baseline, DP mechanisms, evaluation, and best-epsilon selection) can serve as a template for other telemetry or event-log systems where user-level DP is required.

\subsection{Future Work}
Future work includes: extending the evaluation to queries 10 and 11; advanced composition and RDP accounting to tighten the composed guarantee; comparison with other mechanisms (e.g., the exponential mechanism for rankings); and a full-database run of the advance variant at $\epsilon = 0.5$ to confirm the improved guarantee on production-scale data.
